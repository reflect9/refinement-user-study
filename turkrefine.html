Understanding and Improving News Topics
We are researchers from the University of Maryland attempting to measure how people understand themes discovered from newspaper articles, and suggest improvements. Participants of the study are required to be at least 18 years old. If you have any questions about this study, please contact Leah Findlater (http://terpconnect.umd.edu/~leahkf/). Press the button below if you voluntarily agree to participate.

Imagine that you have thousands of New York Times articles published in January 2007, and have to summarize what the articles are about into multiple themes. While the task sounds tedious and time consuming, computers can help.

This screen shows one of the themes that have been automatically identified by the computer, and the news articles that go with that theme.
1. Read over the words in the theme. The computer has grouped the words that appear together in many news articles. This is a "theme". The sizes and order of words represent how frequently they appear in the associated articles.
Practice 1. What is the most frequently appeared word in the theme?
2. Explore the theme and articles. Click a few articles to see full text. Hover the mouse cursor over a word to see some context for where it appears in the articles.
Find the articles that contain the first word. What is the first article of them?
Idealy the theme and articles should be clear and closely related to each other. But, the computer isn't perfect and you may notice problems. Your task is to identify these problems and suggest how to fix them.

Through this HIT, you will see three more themes. For each theme, you will be asked to answer questions about how you understand and improve them. Completing the entire HIT will take around 15 minutes. Please read questions carefully and answer every question.
Participants who were best at improving themes will receive $10 bonus.
